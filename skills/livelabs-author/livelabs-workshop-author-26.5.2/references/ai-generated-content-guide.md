## AI-Generated Content Guide

### 1. Introduction
AI-generated content has the potential to increase efficiency, but it often needs refinement to meet high-quality standards. This document provides guidelines to ensure AI-generated technical marketing content is concise, engaging, and accurate, without fluff, weak phrasing, or unprovable claims.

### 2. Key Goals for AI-Generated Content
Concise: no filler, only strong, direct language.
Active: use strong verbs and avoid soft phrasing.
Precise: avoid vague claims and empty adjectives.
Engaging: use story or context when it helps clarity.

### 3. AI Prompting Guidelines

#### How to Write Effective Prompts
To generate high-quality content, use prompts that enforce clarity and precision. Examples:

- Force active voice and strong verbs:
  - "Write a one-paragraph summary of [feature]. Use short, direct sentences. Avoid adverbs and weak verbs like 'providing' or 'leveraging.' Make every word count."
- Prevent wordiness:
  - "Describe [feature] in under 30 words. Do not use adjectives like 'cutting-edge' or 'innovative.' Every word must add meaning."
- Remove prepositional phrase overload:
  - "Rewrite the following passage in 20% fewer words. Remove unnecessary prepositional phrases and replace weak verbs with stronger ones."
- Encourage storytelling:
  - "Explain how [feature] helped a business solve a real problem. Use a simple narrative format: (1) The problem, (2) The solution, (3) The outcome. Keep it concise."

#### Common Pitfalls and How to Avoid Them
- Wordiness: trim unnecessary words.
- Weak or vague verbs: use stronger action verbs.
- Overuse of prepositional phrases: simplify sentence structure.
- Generic or uninspired tone: add context or a light narrative.
- Inaccuracies or unprovable claims: make every claim fact-based.

### 4. Review Checklist for AI-Generated Content

#### Clarity and Conciseness
- Is every sentence necessary? Eliminate fluff.
- Are unnecessary prepositional phrases removed?
- Can any sentence be shortened without losing meaning?

#### Strong, Active Verbs
- Are weak verbs like `providing`, `leveraging`, and `enabling` replaced with stronger ones?
- Does every verb drive action?

#### Logical Flow and Engagement
- Does the opening sentence ground the reader in a real-world problem or context?
- Is the structure logical: problem -> solution -> outcome?
- Does the introduction talk to the learner's job to be done instead of explaining that the workshop came from a blog, prompt, or idea source?

#### No Marketing Fluff
- Are vague words like `cutting-edge`, `innovative`, or `seamlessly` present? Remove them.
- Does every claim have a factual basis? Remove exaggeration.

#### Accuracy and Technical Rigor
- Are all technical features correctly described? Cross-check them.
- Are there any unprovable claims? Rework or remove them.
- If the content came from source material, does the workshop use that source without naming the authoring process inside learner-facing intro text?

#### External Constraints
- Do not use any of the following words or their roots or synonyms: `guarantee`, `insure`, `ensure`, `assure`.
- Avoid the sentence pattern `<adverb> <weak verb> <noun clause> <gerund> <noun clause>`.

### 5. Implementation Plan
1. Apply structured prompts when generating content.
2. Review output using the checklist.
3. Refine and iterate.
4. Assign responsibility for review and improvement.

### 6. Continuous Improvement
- Measure success with concrete quality metrics.
- Build feedback loops from reviewers.
- Refine prompts and review tactics based on what works.
