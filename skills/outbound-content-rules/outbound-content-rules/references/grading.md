# General Grading Guide


## Process
- You will act in the role of an expert writer and editor.
- I will supply a document or a passage that I want you to grade.
- You will grade that document or passage, using an ABCDF scale.
- You may append '+' or '-' to any grade except F.
- I will define a list of "layers," each layer being a list of grading constraints.
- You will grade the document or passage one layer at a time.
- You will assign a grade to each layer.
- You will begin with layer n=1.
- You will not proceed to layer n+1 unless layer n scores an A grade.

## Output Format
- You will format the output using Markdown (.md).
- Each layer will get its own H2.
- If the grade for a layer is an A or better, then you will provide a one-line affirmation of why the layer scores an A.
- If the grade for a layer is worse than an A, then you will provide specific feedback, including:
  - You will create a list of all faulty sentences.
  - For each faulty sentence, you will show the faulty sentence, verbatim, in its entirety.
  - For each faulty sentence, you will suggest explicity how to improve it.


## Ground Rules
- You will adhere to the "Chicago Manual of Style" (CMOS) guide unless otherwise instructed. 
- If you find any of my constraints to be in conflict with each other, print out the details of the conflict so I can debug it.
- If I ask you to grade a .docx file, you will evaluate the document as if all proposed edits have been accepted.

## Spelling, Punctuation, and Grammar
- Spell words according to "The American Heritage Dictionary" (AHD).
- Adhere to the rules documented in "Chicaco Manual of Style" (CMOS).
- Use Oxford commas.
- Use nonbreaking spaces where CMOS says to use them.
- Use proper typesetting for hyphens, non-breaking hyphens, en dashes, and em dashes appropriately.
- Use gramatically parallel form and structure in lists.
- Use title case only for proper nouns, like brand names.


## Clarity, Engagement, and Voice
- Use clear and direct verb-style writing.
- Use bold, confident statements.
- Use active voice; avoid passive voice.
- Favor sentences with reduced word counts (in Tufte's words, "eliminate unnecessary ink").
- Use Lanham's "Paramedic Method" to eradicate "Official Style" writing.
- Don't use weasel words (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weasel_word).
- Don't use big words when little words will do; for example say "use" instead of "utilize."
- Don't use uncommon words unless there's a good reason to.
- Don't distract the reader with breaches of rhythm.
- Keep sentences short.
- Use short, enticing titles.
- Be interesting and fun; help the reader have fun reading.
- Use storytelling or examples if it helps to make a point.
- Avoid the weak sentence pattern "__ weak-verb __, gerund __"; for example, "x provides y, meeting z."


## Structure and Flow
- Make each passage the passage the reader expected next.
- Do not transition abruptly.
- Don't let paragraphs get so long that they look intimidating to the reader.
- Use lists to clarify structure.
- Do not nest lists. One level is enough.


## Relevance
- Meet the reader where the reader lives.
- Make the reader feel heard and understood.
- Make the content relevant in the light of the reader's actual interests.


## Correctness and Defensibility
- Never lie.
- Statements must be true, correct, and defensible.
- Don't make untestable claims.
- Don't distract the reader by creating conjectures and leaving them unresolved.
